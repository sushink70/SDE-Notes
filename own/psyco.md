# Biological Psychology & Neuroscience: Deep Mastery Roadmap

This is where psychology meets biology—understanding how physical structures and chemical processes generate thoughts, emotions, behaviors, and consciousness itself. This domain requires both memorization and conceptual integration.

---

## **Part 1: Neural Mechanisms - The Language of the Brain**

### **The Neuron: Foundational Unit**

**Core Concepts to Master:**

1. **Neuronal Structure**
   - Dendrites (input receivers), soma (cell body), axon (signal transmitter), terminal buttons (output)
   - Myelin sheath and saltatory conduction (why white matter conducts faster)
   - Glial cells: Not just support—they modulate synaptic transmission, form the blood-brain barrier, and enable neuroplasticity

2. **Action Potential: The Neural "Bit"**
   - **Resting potential** (-70mV): Sodium-potassium pumps maintain electrical gradient
   - **Threshold** (-55mV): Point of no return—voltage-gated sodium channels open
   - **Depolarization → Repolarization → Hyperpolarization**: The all-or-nothing signal
   - **Refractory period**: Prevents backward propagation

   **Mental Model**: Think of neurons as binary switches with a threshold trigger. But unlike computers, the *frequency* of firing (not just on/off) encodes information intensity.

3. **Synaptic Transmission: The Neural "Protocol"**
   - **Electrical synapses** (gap junctions): Fast, bidirectional, rare in humans
   - **Chemical synapses**: Slower but flexible—this is where learning happens
   
   **The Synaptic Sequence** (memorize this cold):
   1. Action potential reaches axon terminal
   2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open
   3. Calcium influx triggers vesicle fusion
   4. Neurotransmitter release into synaptic cleft
   5. Binding to postsynaptic receptors
   6. Ion channels open → EPSP or IPSP
   7. Reuptake or enzymatic degradation terminates signal

4. **Summation & Integration**
   - **Spatial summation**: Multiple synapses fire simultaneously
   - **Temporal summation**: Rapid successive firing from one synapse
   - The axon hillock integrates all inputs → fires if threshold reached

**Deep Understanding Exercise:**
- Why can't action potentials travel backward? (Refractory period + Na+ channel inactivation)
- Why does multiple sclerosis cause motor problems? (Myelin degradation → slowed/failed conduction)
- How do local anesthetics work? (Block voltage-gated Na+ channels → no action potential)

---

## **Part 2: Brain Anatomy - The Architecture of Mind**

### **Learning Strategy: Three-Pass System**

**Pass 1**: Memorize structures and locations (use 3D models, apps like Complete Anatomy)
**Pass 2**: Understand functions and interconnections
**Pass 3**: Clinical correlates (what happens when each area is damaged)

### **Hierarchical Organization**

**Hindbrain (Survival Systems):**
- **Medulla**: Autonomic functions (breathing, heart rate, blood pressure)
- **Pons**: Sleep, arousal, facial movement
- **Cerebellum**: Motor coordination, procedural learning, timing
  - Damage → ataxia (jerky movements), balance issues, slurred speech

**Midbrain (Relay & Reward):**
- **Tectum** (superior & inferior colliculi): Visual and auditory reflexes
- **Tegmentum**: Contains substantia nigra (dopamine) and VTA (reward pathways)
- **Reticular formation**: Arousal, attention, consciousness

**Forebrain (Complex Processing):**

*Subcortical Structures:*
- **Thalamus**: The brain's relay station—almost all sensory info passes through (except smell)
  - Specific nuclei route info to cortex; damage → sensory loss, coma
- **Hypothalamus**: Homeostasis master (hunger, thirst, temperature, circadian rhythm, hormone release)
  - Tiny but critical—damage causes massive dysregulation
- **Basal ganglia** (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus): Motor control, habit formation, reward learning
  - Dysfunction → Parkinson's (dopamine loss), Huntington's (neurodegeneration)
- **Limbic system**:
  - **Amygdala**: Threat detection, fear conditioning, emotional memory
  - **Hippocampus**: Memory consolidation (short-term → long-term), spatial navigation
  - **Cingulate cortex**: Error detection, conflict monitoring, pain processing

*Cerebral Cortex (The Thinking Cap):*

**Four Lobes - Functions & Connections:**

1. **Frontal Lobe**
   - **Primary motor cortex** (M1): Direct motor control (somatotopic map)
   - **Premotor & supplementary motor**: Motor planning, sequencing
   - **Prefrontal cortex** (PFC): Executive functions—planning, decision-making, impulse control, working memory
     - **Dorsolateral PFC**: Cognitive control, reasoning
     - **Ventromedial PFC**: Emotion regulation, value-based decisions
     - **Orbitofrontal cortex**: Reward processing, social behavior
   - *Damage*: Phineas Gage case—personality changes, impulsivity, loss of social inhibition

2. **Parietal Lobe**
   - **Primary somatosensory cortex** (S1): Touch, temperature, pain perception
   - **Posterior parietal**: Spatial processing, attention, sensorimotor integration
   - *Damage*: Hemispatial neglect (ignore half of visual field), apraxia (can't perform learned movements)

3. **Temporal Lobe**
   - **Primary auditory cortex** (A1): Sound processing
   - **Wernicke's area**: Language comprehension
   - **Inferior temporal**: Object recognition (face areas, "what" pathway)
   - *Damage*: Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but meaningless speech), prosopagnosia (can't recognize faces)

4. **Occipital Lobe**
   - **Primary visual cortex** (V1): Basic visual processing (edges, orientation)
   - **Extrastriate areas** (V2-V5): Color, motion, form
   - *Damage*: Cortical blindness, motion blindness (akinetopsia), achromatopsia

**Lateralization: Left vs Right Hemisphere**
- **Left**: Language (95% of right-handers), analytical processing, sequential thinking
- **Right**: Spatial processing, face recognition, emotional prosody, holistic processing
- Connected by **corpus callosum** (split-brain studies reveal specialization)

**Mental Model: Dual Processing Streams**
- **Ventral "What" pathway**: Occipital → Temporal (object identification)
- **Dorsal "Where/How" pathway**: Occipital → Parietal (spatial location, motor guidance)

## **Part 3: Neurotransmitter Systems - The Brain's Chemical Language**

### **Major Neurotransmitter Families**

**1. Amino Acids (Fast Transmission)**

**Glutamate (Excitatory - 80% of brain signaling)**
- Primary excitatory neurotransmitter
- Critical for learning/memory via **long-term potentiation (LTP)**
- Receptors: AMPA (fast), NMDA (slow, voltage-dependent, requires coincidence detection)
- *Excess*: Excitotoxicity (stroke, seizures, neurodegenerative diseases)

**GABA (Inhibitory - 20% of brain signaling)**
- Primary inhibitory neurotransmitter
- Balances excitation, prevents runaway neural activity
- **GABA-A receptors**: Fast inhibition (target of benzodiazepines, alcohol)
- **GABA-B receptors**: Slower, modulatory
- *Dysfunction*: Anxiety disorders, epilepsy

**Mental Model**: Glutamate is the accelerator, GABA is the brake. Brain function requires precise balance.

---

**2. Monoamines (Modulatory Systems)**

**Dopamine (DA) - The "Want" System**
- **Pathways**:
  - *Mesolimbic*: VTA → nucleus accumbens (reward, motivation, addiction)
  - *Mesocortical*: VTA → prefrontal cortex (executive function, working memory)
  - *Nigrostriatal*: Substantia nigra → striatum (motor control)
  - *Tuberoinfundibular*: Hypothalamus → pituitary (prolactin regulation)
  
- **Functions**: Reward prediction error, motivation, motor control, attention
- **Dysfunction**:
  - *Excess*: Schizophrenia positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions)
  - *Deficit*: Parkinson's disease (nigrostriatal), depression (mesocortical)
- **Drugs**: Cocaine (blocks reuptake), amphetamine (releases DA), antipsychotics (block D2 receptors)

**Serotonin (5-HT) - The "Stability" System**
- Synthesized in **raphe nuclei**, projects throughout brain
- **Functions**: Mood regulation, sleep, appetite, impulse control, social behavior
- **Dysfunction**: Depression, anxiety, OCD
- **Receptors**: 14+ subtypes (5-HT1A, 5-HT2A most studied)
- **Drugs**: SSRIs (block reuptake), psychedelics (5-HT2A agonists), triptans for migraines

**Norepinephrine (NE) - The "Arousal" System**
- Synthesized in **locus coeruleus**
- **Functions**: Alertness, attention, stress response, vigilance
- **Peripheral**: Sympathetic nervous system activation (fight-or-flight)
- **Dysfunction**: ADHD (insufficient NE), PTSD (hyperarousal)
- **Drugs**: Stimulants (increase NE), SNRIs, beta-blockers (block NE in periphery)

**Critical Insight**: These systems don't work in isolation—they interact constantly. Dopamine-serotonin balance affects mood; NE-dopamine interaction modulates attention.

---

**3. Acetylcholine (ACh) - The "Focus" System**
- **Locations**: Basal forebrain → cortex/hippocampus; brainstem → thalamus
- **Functions**: 
  - *Central*: Attention, learning, memory consolidation, cortical activation
  - *Peripheral*: Muscle contraction (neuromuscular junction)
- **Dysfunction**: Alzheimer's disease (cholinergic neuron loss), myasthenia gravis (ACh receptor antibodies)
- **Drugs**: Nicotine (receptor agonist), donepezil (increases ACh in Alzheimer's), curare (blocks receptors)

---

**4. Neuropeptides (Slow, Long-Lasting)**

**Endogenous Opioids (Endorphins, Enkephalins)**
- Pain modulation, reward, stress response
- Receptors: μ, δ, κ (mu-opioid = morphine target)

**Oxytocin & Vasopressin**
- Social bonding, trust, pair bonding, maternal behavior
- Vasopressin: Aggression, territorial behavior (especially males)

**Substance P**
- Pain transmission, inflammation

**Mental Model**: Neuropeptides are like hormones—they modulate neural circuits over longer timescales, setting the "mood" of brain regions.

---

## **Part 4: Psychopharmacology Basics**

### **Core Principles**

**1. Drug Action Mechanisms**
- **Agonist**: Activates receptor (mimics neurotransmitter)
- **Antagonist**: Blocks receptor (prevents activation)
- **Reuptake inhibitor**: Increases neurotransmitter availability (SSRIs, cocaine)
- **Enzyme inhibitor**: Prevents degradation (MAOIs block monoamine oxidase)
- **Release enhancer**: Triggers neurotransmitter release (amphetamine)

**2. Pharmacokinetics (What the body does to the drug)**
- **Absorption**: How drug enters bloodstream
- **Distribution**: Blood-brain barrier permeability
- **Metabolism**: Liver breakdown (cytochrome P450 enzymes)
- **Excretion**: Kidneys eliminate drug

**3. Pharmacodynamics (What the drug does to the body)**
- **Dose-response curve**: Relationship between dose and effect
- **Therapeutic window**: Range between effective dose and toxic dose
- **Tolerance**: Decreased response with repeated use
- **Dependence**: Physical/psychological need
- **Withdrawal**: Symptoms when drug discontinued

### **Major Drug Classes**

**Antidepressants:**
- **SSRIs** (fluoxetine, sertraline): Block serotonin reuptake
- **SNRIs** (venlafaxine): Block serotonin + norepinephrine reuptake
- **TCAs** (amitriptyline): Block multiple neurotransmitters (more side effects)
- **MAOIs** (phenelzine): Block monoamine oxidase (dietary restrictions)
- *Lag time*: 2-6 weeks for effects (neuroplastic changes, not just neurotransmitter increase)

**Antipsychotics:**
- **Typical** (haloperidol): D2 antagonists (motor side effects)
- **Atypical** (risperidone, clozapine): D2 + 5-HT2A antagonists (fewer motor issues)
- Treat positive symptoms (hallucinations) better than negative symptoms (flat affect)

**Anxiolytics:**
- **Benzodiazepines** (diazepam, alprazolam): GABA-A positive allosteric modulators
  - Fast-acting but risk of dependence
- **Buspirone**: 5-HT1A partial agonist (no dependence risk)

**Stimulants:**
- **Methylphenidate** (Ritalin): Blocks dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake
- **Amphetamine** (Adderall): Releases dopamine/norepinephrine
- Used for ADHD; abuse potential via mesolimbic dopamine activation

## **Part 5: Evolutionary Psychology Foundations**

### **Core Logic: Natural Selection Shaped the Brain**

**Fundamental Principles:**

1. **Adaptation**: Traits that increased reproductive success were selected
2. **Mismatch Theory**: Modern environment ≠ ancestral environment
   - Craving sugar/fat made sense in scarcity; causes obesity in abundance
   - Fear of snakes/spiders adaptive; fear of cars (deadlier) isn't innate

3. **Modularity Hypothesis**: Mind contains specialized modules for specific problems
   - Face recognition module (fusiform face area)
   - Language acquisition device (Chomsky)
   - Theory of mind module (understanding others' mental states)

**Key Evolutionary Adaptations:**

**Social Brain Hypothesis:**
- Large human brain evolved for navigating complex social relationships
- **Dunbar's number**: ~150 stable social relationships (related to neocortex size)
- Social exclusion activates pain circuits (anterior cingulate) → ostracism feels physically painful

**Sexual Selection:**
- **Mate preferences**: Men prefer youth/fertility cues; women prefer resources/status (parental investment theory)
- **Intrasexual competition**: Aggression, dominance displays
- **Intersexual selection**: Traits that attract mates (e.g., humor, creativity)

**Parental Investment Theory:**
- Sex that invests more (usually females) is choosier
- Explains sex differences in mating strategies, jealousy (sexual vs emotional infidelity)

**Kin Selection & Altruism:**
- "Selfish gene" perspective (Dawkins): Help relatives who share your genes
- **Hamilton's rule**: Altruism when rb > c (relatedness × benefit > cost)

**Evolutionary Emotions:**
- **Fear**: Threat detection (amygdala-based, fast, unconscious)
- **Disgust**: Pathogen avoidance (contaminated food, disease cues)
- **Jealousy**: Protect reproductive investment
- **Shame/guilt**: Enforce social norms, cooperation

**Critical Thinking Required:**
- Evolutionary explanations are **hypotheses**, not proven facts
- Risk of **just-so stories**: Making up plausible but untestable narratives
- **Proximate vs. ultimate causes**: How mechanisms work vs. why they evolved
- Culture massively influences behavior—biology isn't destiny

## **Integration: How These Domains Connect**

**Example: Depression**
- **Neural**: Reduced hippocampal volume, decreased PFC activity, amygdala hyperactivity
- **Neurotransmitter**: Low serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine; glutamate-GABA imbalance
- **Pharmacology**: SSRIs increase serotonin → neuroplasticity (BDNF) → symptom relief
- **Evolutionary**: Depression may be adaptive in some contexts (analytical rumination hypothesis) or a mismatch with modern stressors

**Example: PTSD**
- **Neural**: Amygdala hyperresponsivity, reduced hippocampal function, impaired PFC regulation
- **Neurotransmitter**: Elevated NE (hyperarousal), dysregulated cortisol
- **Evolutionary**: Fear conditioning = adaptive survival mechanism; PTSD = maladaptive overgeneralization

# Cognitive Psychology: Deep Mastery Roadmap

Cognitive psychology is the science of **information processing**—how the brain encodes, transforms, stores, retrieves, and uses information. Think of it as reverse-engineering the algorithms of human cognition.

---

## **Part 1: Perception - Constructing Reality from Chaos**

### **The Fundamental Problem: Inverse Optics**

The retina receives a 2D projection of a 3D world. Perception is the brain's **inference** about what caused that sensory input. This is an **ill-posed problem**—infinite 3D scenes could produce the same 2D image.

**Core Principle**: *Perception is not passive recording; it's active construction based on prior knowledge and assumptions.*

### **Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Processing**

**Bottom-Up (Data-Driven):**
- Sensory input → feature extraction → pattern recognition
- Example: Detecting edges, colors, motion in visual cortex

**Top-Down (Concept-Driven):**
- Prior knowledge, expectations, context influence perception
- Example: Reading "TAE CAT" vs "THE CAT"—you fill in the H automatically

**Mental Model**: Perception = **Bayesian inference**
- Prior beliefs + sensory evidence → posterior belief (percept)
- The brain constantly predicts incoming sensory input and updates when predictions fail
- This is **predictive coding theory**—the dominant framework in modern neuroscience

---

### **Visual Perception: From Photons to Phenomenology**

**Early Visual Processing (V1):**
- **Feature detectors**: Simple cells (edges, orientation), complex cells (movement)
- **Retinotopic mapping**: Spatial organization preserved
- Discovered by Hubel & Wiesel (Nobel Prize)—single-neuron recordings in cats

**Gestalt Principles of Perceptual Organization:**
*(The whole is different from the sum of its parts)*

1. **Figure-Ground**: Segregating objects from background
2. **Proximity**: Elements close together are grouped
3. **Similarity**: Similar elements are grouped
4. **Continuity**: We perceive continuous patterns
5. **Closure**: We complete incomplete figures
6. **Common Fate**: Elements moving together are grouped

**Why These Exist**: Evolution hardwired heuristics that work in natural environments—objects are usually continuous, coherent, and move together.

**Depth Perception (How we see 3D in 2D):**

*Binocular Cues:*
- **Retinal disparity**: Slightly different images from each eye
- **Convergence**: Eye muscle tension when focusing on near objects

*Monocular Cues:*
- **Linear perspective**: Parallel lines converge
- **Texture gradient**: Texture becomes denser with distance
- **Interposition**: Near objects occlude far objects
- **Relative size**: Smaller image = farther object (given same actual size)
- **Height in visual field**: Objects higher up appear farther
- **Motion parallax**: Near objects move faster across retina

**Perceptual Constancies** (Stability despite changing input):
- **Size constancy**: Object's perceived size stays constant despite retinal image size changes
- **Shape constancy**: Door appears rectangular even when viewed at angle
- **Brightness constancy**: White paper appears white in shadow or sunlight
- **Color constancy**: Banana appears yellow under different lighting

**Classic Illusions Reveal Processing Rules:**

- **Müller-Lyer illusion**: Lines with inward/outward arrows appear different lengths
  - *Why*: Depth cue misapplication—corners interpreted as 3D
- **Ponzo illusion**: Converging lines make upper object appear larger
  - *Why*: Linear perspective cue triggers size scaling
- **Ames room**: People appear to shrink/grow
  - *Why*: Brain assumes rectangular room, not trapezoidal

**Deep Insight**: Illusions aren't failures—they reveal the heuristics the brain uses. These shortcuts work 99% of the time in natural environments.

---

### **Object Recognition: The Binding Problem**

**How does the brain integrate color, shape, motion, depth into unified objects?**

**Feature Integration Theory (Treisman):**
- **Preattentive stage**: Parallel processing of basic features (color, orientation, motion)
- **Focused attention stage**: Features bound together by attention to create objects
- Without attention → **illusory conjunctions** (red square + blue circle → red circle)

**Recognition-by-Components Theory (Biederman):**
- Objects decomposed into basic shapes called **geons** (cylinders, cones, blocks)
- ~36 geons combine to form all objects
- View-invariant (recognize objects from different angles)

**Face Recognition (Special Case):**
- **Fusiform Face Area (FFA)**: Specialized face processing region
- **Holistic processing**: Faces processed as wholes, not parts (face inversion effect)
- **Prosopagnosia**: Face blindness from FFA damage (can't recognize faces but can recognize other objects)

---

## **Part 2: Attention - The Cognitive Spotlight**

### **The Core Problem: Limited Capacity**

The brain receives ~11 million bits/second of sensory input but can consciously process only ~50 bits/second. **Attention** is the selection mechanism.

### **Types of Attention**

**1. Selective Attention (Filtering)**
- Focus on one stimulus while ignoring others
- **Cocktail party effect**: Hear your name across crowded room despite filtering

**Classic Studies:**

**Dichotic Listening (Cherry, 1953):**
- Different messages to each ear
- Shadow (repeat aloud) one ear's message
- Participants remember almost nothing from unattended ear
- BUT: Detect their name, gender switch, language change

**Implication**: Unattended information isn't completely blocked—some semantic processing occurs.

**2. Divided Attention (Multitasking)**
- Performing multiple tasks simultaneously
- **Dual-task interference**: Performance drops when tasks compete for resources
- Works well only when tasks use different modalities or are automatic

**3. Sustained Attention (Vigilance)**
- Maintaining focus over time
- **Vigilance decrement**: Performance degrades after 20-30 minutes
- Critical for air traffic control, security screening, driving

---

### **Attention Theories: Evolution of Understanding**

**Early Selection (Broadbent's Filter Model, 1958):**
- Sensory input → bottleneck filter (selects by physical features) → semantic processing
- Predicts: Unattended info never reaches awareness
- Problem: Doesn't explain cocktail party effect

**Late Selection (Deutsch & Deutsch, 1963):**
- All inputs processed semantically → selection occurs at response stage
- Predicts: Everything reaches awareness before selection
- Problem: Wastes processing resources

**Attenuation Model (Treisman, 1964):**
- **Compromise**: Filter attenuates (weakens) unattended input instead of blocking
- Important stimuli (name, danger words) have lower thresholds
- Explains cocktail party effect

**Modern View: Flexible Attention**
- Selection can occur at multiple stages (early or late) depending on task demands and perceptual load
- **Perceptual Load Theory (Lavie)**: High perceptual load → early selection (no resources for unattended); low load → late selection

---

### **Attention as a Spotlight vs. Zoom Lens**

**Spotlight Metaphor:**
- Attention illuminates a region of space
- Information inside spotlight processed; outside ignored
- **Covert attention**: Attention shifts without eye movement

**Zoom Lens Metaphor:**
- Attention can adjust focus from narrow (high resolution, small area) to broad (low resolution, large area)
- Trade-off between scope and precision

**Spatial Attention Paradigm (Posner Cueing):**
- Cue indicates likely target location
- **Valid cue**: Target appears at cued location → faster RT
- **Invalid cue**: Target appears elsewhere → slower RT
- Shows attention can shift before eye movement

---

### **Inattentional Blindness & Change Blindness**

**Inattentional Blindness:**
- Failure to notice unexpected stimulus when attention focused elsewhere
- **Gorilla study (Simons & Chabris)**: 50% of observers fail to see gorilla walking through basketball players

**Change Blindness:**
- Failure to detect large changes in scene during interruption (blink, saccade, cut)
- Shows we don't have complete internal representation of visual world

**Philosophical Implication**: Conscious awareness requires attention. Without attention, information may be processed but remains unconscious.

---

## **Part 3: Memory Systems - The Architecture of Experience**

### **The Multi-Store Model (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968)**

Classic framework—still foundational:

**Sensory Memory → Short-Term Memory → Long-Term Memory**

---

### **Sensory Memory: The Briefest Echo**

**Iconic Memory (Visual):**
- Duration: ~0.5 seconds
- Capacity: High (entire visual field)
- **Sperling's Partial Report Paradigm**: Flash 12 letters briefly → can report ~4; but if cued immediately to report one row → can report that row perfectly
- *Implication*: Information decays rapidly; we can access more than we can report

**Echoic Memory (Auditory):**
- Duration: ~3-4 seconds (longer than visual)
- Allows us to replay last few seconds of speech ("What did you say?" → then realize you heard it)

---

### **Short-Term Memory (STM) / Working Memory**

**STM (Original Concept):**
- Duration: ~15-30 seconds without rehearsal
- Capacity: **7 ± 2 items** (Miller's magic number)
- **Chunking**: Group items into meaningful units (phone number: 555-1234 = 2 chunks, not 7 digits)

**Working Memory (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974):**
*More sophisticated model—STM is active workspace:*

**Components:**

1. **Phonological Loop** (Verbal/auditory information)
   - **Phonological store**: Holds sounds for ~2 seconds
   - **Articulatory rehearsal**: Subvocal repetition refreshes store
   - **Word length effect**: Shorter words easier to remember (fit more in 2-second loop)
   - **Articulatory suppression**: Repeating "the the the" disrupts rehearsal

2. **Visuospatial Sketchpad** (Visual/spatial information)
   - Holds mental images and spatial layouts
   - Separate from phonological loop (can do visual + verbal simultaneously)

3. **Central Executive** (Attention controller)
   - Allocates attention between subsystems
   - Switches between tasks
   - Updates and manipulates information
   - Linked to prefrontal cortex (PFC)
   - **Dysexecutive syndrome**: PFC damage → poor planning, impulsivity, task-switching deficits

4. **Episodic Buffer** (Later addition)
   - Integrates information from subsystems and LTM
   - Creates unified multimodal representations

**Mental Model**: Working memory is RAM—actively holding and manipulating information for current tasks.

---

### **Long-Term Memory (LTM): The Infinite Library**

**Capacity**: Essentially unlimited (no known upper bound)
**Duration**: Lifetime (though retrieval may fail)

**Major Divisions:**

**1. Explicit/Declarative Memory** (Conscious, can be verbalized)

**A. Episodic Memory** (Personal experiences)
- What: Specific events in your life
- When/Where: Contextual information (time, place)
- Examples: Your last birthday, first day of school
- Brain: Hippocampus (consolidation), medial temporal lobe
- **Patient H.M.**: Bilateral hippocampus removal → couldn't form new episodic memories (anterograde amnesia) but retained old memories (retrograde sparing)

**B. Semantic Memory** (General knowledge)
- Facts, concepts, meanings
- Decontextualized (don't remember when/where you learned it)
- Examples: Paris is capital of France, apples are fruit
- Brain: Lateral temporal cortex, anterior temporal lobe
- Can be intact even when episodic memory impaired

**2. Implicit/Nondeclarative Memory** (Unconscious, automatic)

**A. Procedural Memory** (Skills, habits)
- How to ride bike, type, play piano
- Brain: Basal ganglia, cerebellum, motor cortex
- **Patient H.M.**: Could learn new motor skills (mirror tracing) despite no conscious memory of practice
- *Implication*: Declarative and procedural systems are dissociable

**B. Priming** (Facilitation from prior exposure)
- Seeing "doctor" makes "nurse" easier to recognize
- Faster processing of previously encountered stimuli
- Brain: Neocortex (different regions for different types)

**C. Conditioning** (Associative learning)
- Classical conditioning (Pavlov): CS + UCS → CR
- Brain: Amygdala (emotional conditioning), cerebellum (motor conditioning)

---

### **Memory Processes: Encoding → Storage → Retrieval**

**Encoding (Getting information in):**

**Levels of Processing (Craik & Lockhart):**
- **Shallow**: Physical features (font, sound)
- **Deep**: Meaning, semantic processing
- *Deep processing → better memory*
- Example: Remember words better if you judge pleasantness vs. count letters

**Encoding Specificity Principle:**
- Memory best when retrieval context matches encoding context
- **Context-dependent memory**: Study underwater → recall better underwater (Godden & Baddeley)
- **State-dependent memory**: Drunk while learning → recall better while drunk

**Self-Reference Effect:**
- Information related to self encoded most deeply
- "Does this word describe you?" → best recall

**Spacing Effect:**
- Distributed practice > massed practice (cramming)
- **Spaced repetition systems** (Anki) exploit this

---

**Storage (Maintaining information):**

**Consolidation:**
- Process of stabilizing memory traces
- **Synaptic consolidation**: Minutes to hours (LTP, protein synthesis)
- **Systems consolidation**: Months to years (hippocampus → cortex transfer)
- **Sleep critical**: Memory consolidation occurs during slow-wave sleep (declarative) and REM (procedural)

**Reconsolidation:**
- Memories become labile when retrieved, must be re-stored
- Explains **memory distortion**: Each retrieval can modify the memory
- **Misinformation effect** (Loftus): Post-event information alters original memory

---

**Retrieval (Getting information out):**

**Recall vs. Recognition:**
- **Recall**: Generate information from memory (essay exam)
- **Recognition**: Identify previously encountered information (multiple choice)
- Recognition usually easier (requires only familiarity, not complete reconstruction)

**Retrieval Cues:**
- Prompts that trigger memory access
- **Tip-of-the-tongue state**: Know you know it, can't access it
- Partial information (first letter) often triggers recall

**Retrieval-Induced Forgetting:**
- Practicing some items inhibits recall of related unpracticed items
- Example: Repeatedly retrieving "fruit-banana" makes "fruit-orange" harder to recall

**Testing Effect:**
- Retrieval practice (testing) strengthens memory more than re-studying
- Active recall > passive review

---

### **Why We Forget**

**1. Encoding Failure** (Never stored in first place)
- Most "forgetting" is failure to encode
- Inattention → no storage

**2. Storage Decay** (Trace deteriorates over time)
- **Ebbinghaus forgetting curve**: Rapid initial forgetting, then levels off
- Controversial—some argue memories don't decay, only become harder to retrieve

**3. Retrieval Failure** (Information stored but inaccessible)
- Lack of appropriate cues
- **Cue-dependent forgetting**: Context/state mismatch

**4. Interference**
- **Proactive interference**: Old learning interferes with new (old phone number blocks new one)
- **Retroactive interference**: New learning interferes with old (new phone number blocks old one)
- Occurs when memories share retrieval cues

**5. Motivated Forgetting** (Repression—controversial)
- Freudian concept: Unconsciously suppress traumatic memories
- Limited empirical support; most trauma survivors remember too well (PTSD)

---

### **Memory Distortions: Reconstructive Nature**

**Bartlett's War of the Ghosts:**
- Participants read unfamiliar story, recalled it later
- Systematic distortions: Made story fit their cultural schemas
- *Conclusion*: Memory is reconstruction, not reproduction

**Schema Theory:**
- Schemas (mental frameworks) guide encoding and retrieval
- Fill in gaps with schema-consistent information
- **False memories**: "Remember" events that never happened but fit schemas

**Misinformation Effect (Loftus):**
- Post-event information incorporated into memory
- "How fast were cars going when they smashed?" → higher speed estimates + false memory of broken glass
- Legal implications: Eyewitness testimony unreliable

**Source Monitoring Errors:**
- Confuse source of information (did I see it or imagine it?)
- **Cryptomnesia**: Unintentional plagiarism (think you created idea but actually heard it)

**Flashbulb Memories:**
- Vivid, detailed memories of surprising/emotional events (9/11, JFK assassination)
- Feel confident and detailed BUT equally susceptible to errors
- Emotional arousal enhances *confidence*, not accuracy

---

## **Part 4: Decision-Making - Rationality and Its Limits**

### **The Rational Agent Model (Classical Economics)**

Assumes humans:
1. Have stable, consistent preferences
2. Know all options and their outcomes
3. Calculate expected utility for each option
4. Choose option that maximizes utility

**Reality**: We violate all of these constantly.

---

### **Heuristics and Biases (Kahneman & Tversky)**

**Heuristics** = Mental shortcuts that reduce cognitive effort

**1. Representativeness Heuristic**
- Judge probability by similarity to prototype
- **Linda problem**: "Linda is 31, single, outspoken, majored in philosophy, concerned with discrimination and social justice. Which is more probable?
  - (A) Linda is a bank teller
  - (B) Linda is a bank teller and active in feminist movement"
- Most choose B—**conjunction fallacy** (P(A and B) cannot exceed P(A))
- Violates logic because B is more "representative" of description

**2. Availability Heuristic**
- Judge frequency/probability by ease of recall
- **Examples**:
  - Overestimate shark attacks (vivid, media coverage) vs. vending machine deaths (more common)
  - After plane crash, people avoid flying (availability increased)
- **Bias toward**: Recent, vivid, emotional events

**3. Anchoring and Adjustment**
- Initial value (anchor) disproportionately influences estimates
- **Experiment**: Spin wheel (random number 10 or 65) → estimate % African countries in UN
  - Those who saw 10: Average estimate ~25%
  - Those who saw 65: Average estimate ~45%
- Occurs even with obviously irrelevant anchors

---

### **Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky - Nobel Prize)**

Descriptive theory of how people actually make decisions under risk.

**Key Principles:**

**1. Reference Dependence**
- Evaluate outcomes relative to reference point (usually status quo)
- Not absolute wealth but *gains* and *losses* matter

**2. Loss Aversion**
- Losses loom larger than equivalent gains
- Losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good
- Ratio: ~2:1 (losses hurt twice as much)
- **Endowment effect**: Value possessions more once owned (selling price > buying price)

**3. Diminishing Sensitivity**
- Marginal impact of gains/losses decreases
- $100 → $200 feels better than $1000 → $1100
- Creates S-shaped value function (concave for gains, convex for losses)

**4. Probability Weighting**
- Overweight small probabilities (lottery tickets, insurance)
- Underweight moderate-high probabilities
- **Certainty effect**: Preference for certainty over high probability

**Applications:**
- **Framing effects**: "90% survival rate" more appealing than "10% mortality rate" (same info, different frame)
- **Status quo bias**: Prefer current state (loss aversion → changes feel risky)
- **Sunk cost fallacy**: Continue failing project because of past investment (should be irrelevant to future decisions)

---

### **Dual-Process Theory (System 1 vs System 2)**

**System 1: Fast, Automatic, Intuitive**
- Operates unconsciously, effortlessly
- Pattern recognition, associative
- Emotionally laden
- Examples: Recognize face, understand simple sentences, drive on empty road
- **Strengths**: Quick, efficient
- **Weaknesses**: Biased, error-prone

**System 2: Slow, Controlled, Deliberate**
- Requires conscious attention, effort
- Logical, rule-based
- Emotionally neutral
- Examples: Solve math problem, compare products, park in tight space
- **Strengths**: Accurate, logical
- **Weaknesses**: Slow, limited capacity

**Relationship:**
- System 2 is lazy—relies on System 1 when possible
- Cognitive load reduces System 2 engagement → more bias
- **Ego depletion**: Mental effort depletes willpower (controversial—replication issues)

**Mental Model**: System 1 generates quick intuitions; System 2 monitors and sometimes overrides them.

---

### **Confirmation Bias**

- Seek information confirming existing beliefs
- Ignore or rationalize disconfirming evidence
- **Wason selection task**: Test rule "If card has vowel on one side, must have even number on other"
  - Cards showing: E, K, 4, 7
  - Which to flip? Most say E and 4 (confirmation)
  - Correct: E and 7 (could disconfirm if 7 has vowel on back)

**Why it matters**: Leads to polarization, poor decision-making, pseudoscience acceptance

---

## **Part 5: Problem-Solving - From Impasse to Insight**

### **Problem Space Theory (Newell & Simon)**

**Problem**: Initial state → Goal state
**Problem space**: All possible states and operators (actions)
**Solution**: Path through problem space from initial to goal

**Example: Tower of Hanoi**
- Initial: All disks on peg A
- Goal: All disks on peg C
- Operators: Move one disk at a time, never place larger on smaller

---

### **Problem-Solving Strategies**

**1. Algorithms**
- Systematic procedures guaranteeing solution
- Exhaustive but slow
- Example: Try every possible combination for lock

**2. Heuristics**
- Rules of thumb that usually work
- Fast but no guarantee

**Types:**

**Means-End Analysis:**
- Reduce difference between current state and goal
- Break into subgoals
- Example: GPS navigation—identify closest step toward destination

**Working Backward:**
- Start from goal, work toward initial state
- Useful when goal constraints clearer than start
- Example: Proof-writing in mathematics

**Analogical Problem-Solving:**
- Apply solution from similar problem
- **Duncker's radiation problem**: Destroy tumor with rays without harming tissue → use multiple weak rays converging on tumor
- **Transfer**: Most people don't spontaneously notice analogy unless explicitly cued
- **Surface vs. Structural similarity**: Experts recognize structural similarity; novices distracted by surface features

---

### **Insight vs. Incremental Problem-Solving**

**Insight (Aha! moment):**
- Sudden realization of solution
- **Restructuring**: Represent problem in new way
- Feels qualitatively different from step-by-step solving

**Classic Problems:**

**9-Dot Problem:**
- Connect all dots with 4 straight lines without lifting pen
- Requires going outside implicit boundary (most assume must stay within dot rectangle)

**Candle Problem (Duncker):**
- Attach candle to wall using only candle, box of tacks, matches
- Solution: Empty box, tack box to wall as platform
- **Functional fixedness**: Seeing box only as container, not potential platform
- Overcome by: Presenting box empty (not as tack container)

**Two-String Problem:**
- Tie together two ceiling strings too far apart to reach both
- Solution: Tie weight to one, swing it like pendulum while holding other
- Requires seeing pliers as weight, not tool

**Fixation Types:**
- **Functional fixedness**: Can't see alternative uses for objects
- **Mental set**: Persist with familiar strategy even when inefficient (Luchins' water jug problems)

---

### **Expertise Development**

**Characteristics of Experts:**

1. **Superior domain-specific knowledge**
   - Not just more facts—organized differently
   - **Chunking**: Recognize meaningful patterns
   - **Chase & Simon chess study**: Experts recall actual game positions better but no advantage for random positions

2. **Deeper representations**
   - Novices: Surface features
   - Experts: Underlying principles
   - **Physics problems**: Novices group by surface (inclined planes); experts by principle (conservation of energy)

3. **Automatic pattern recognition**
   - Free up working memory for strategy
   - **10,000-hour rule** (Ericsson): ~10 years of deliberate practice to reach expertise
   - Quality matters more than quantity

4. **Superior metacognition**
   - Better at monitoring own understanding
   - Know what they don't know

**Mental Model**: Expertise = vast pattern library + automatic retrieval + strategic thinking

---

## **Part 6: Reasoning - Logic and Its Violations**

### **Deductive Reasoning (Specific conclusions from general premises)**

**Syllogisms:**
- All A are B
- All B are C
- Therefore, all A are C (valid)

**Common Errors:**
- **Belief bias**: Accept conclusion if believable, reject if unbelievable (regardless of logical validity)
  - "All flowers have petals. Roses have petals. Therefore roses are flowers." (Invalid but believable)
- **Atmosphere effect**: Premises with "some" lead to "some" conclusion

**Conditional Reasoning:**
- If P then Q
- **Modus ponens** (valid): P is true → Q is true
- **Modus tollens** (valid): Q is false → P is false
- **Affirming consequent** (invalid): Q is true → P is true (?)
- **Denying antecedent** (invalid): P is false → Q is false (?)

People good at modus ponens, poor at modus tollens.

---

### **Inductive Reasoning (General conclusions from specific observations)**

**No guarantee of certainty**—observations may be unrepresentative

**Confirmation bias** especially pernicious here
- Seek confirming cases, ignore disconfirming

**Base rate neglect:**
- Ignore prior probabilities
- **Medical diagnosis**: Rare disease test 95% accurate
  - Disease occurs in 1/1000 people
  - You test positive
  - What's probability you have disease?
  - Most say 95%—**wrong**
  - Correct: ~2% (need to consider base rate)

---

### **Analogical Reasoning**

- Map relationships from source domain to target domain
- **Structure mapping** (Gentner): Align relational structure, not surface features
- **Example**: "Atom is like solar system" (nucleus:electrons :: sun:planets)

**Limitations:**
- Analogies can mislead if mapping is superficial
- "Brain is like computer" analogy—useful but breaks down (brain is massively parallel, plastic, analog)

---

## **Part 7: Language and Thought - The Mind's Expression**

### **Language Structure (Hierarchical)**

**Phonemes** (sound units) → **Morphemes** (meaning units) → **Words** → **Phrases** → **Sentences** → **Discourse**

**Phonemes:**
- Smallest units that change meaning (/b/ vs /p/ in "bat" vs "pat")
- ~44 in English; varies by language

**Morphemes:**
- Smallest meaningful units
- "Unbreakable" = un- (not) + break + -able (can be) = 3 morphemes

**Syntax (Grammar):**
- Rules for combining words into sentences
- **Phrase structure**: Sentences have hierarchical tree structure
  - "The dog chased the cat" → [NP The dog] [VP chased [NP the cat]]
- **Chomsky**: Universal Grammar—innate language acquisition device
  - Evidence: Children acquire complex syntax without explicit teaching

**Semantics (Meaning):**
- **Lexical**: Word meanings
- **Compositional**: Sentence meaning from word meanings + syntax
- **Ambiguity**: 
  - Lexical: "Bank" (financial institution vs. river edge)
  - Syntactic: "Visiting relatives can be annoying" (you visit them vs. they visit you)

**Pragmatics (Context, use):**
- Conversational implicature (Grice's maxims): Cooperative principle, relevance
- "Can you pass the salt?" → request, not yes/no question

---

### **Language Comprehension**

**Word Recognition:**
- **Interactive activation model** (McClelland & Rumelhart):
  - Feature level → Letter level → Word level
  - Bidirectional excitation and inhibition
  - Explains word superiority effect (recognize letters better in words than isolation)

**Sentence Processing:**
- **Incremental**: Process word-by-word, not waiting for full sentence
- **Garden path sentences**: "The horse raced past the barn fell"
  - Initially parse "raced" as main verb → need to revise (should be reduced relative clause)
  - Shows commitment to early interpretation

**Discourse Comprehension:**
- Build **situation model**: Mental representation of described situation
- Track **anaphora** (pronouns, references)
- Make **inferences**: Bridging (connect sentences), elaborative (enrich with world knowledge)

---

### **Language Production**

**Stages (Levelt):**
1. **Conceptualization**: Decide what to say
2. **Formulation**: Select words, build syntactic structure
3. **Articulation**: Execute motor commands for speech

**Speech Errors Reveal Processing:**
- **Spoonerisms**: Exchange initial sounds ("You've hissed my mystery lectures")
- **Freudian slips**: Unintended meaningful errors (controversial)
- Errors respect linguistic constraints (exchange words of same grammatical category)

**Tip-of-the-Tongue (TOT) state:**
- Know word, can't retrieve it
- Often have partial phonological info (first letter, syllable count)
- Shows separation of semantic and phonological access

---

### **Language and Thought: Do They Shape Each Other?**

**Linguistic Relativity (Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis):**

**Strong version** (Linguistic Determinism): Language determines thought—can't think concepts without words
- **Mostly rejected**: Can think without language (prelinguistic infants, animals think)

**Weak version**: Language influences thought
- **Some evidence**:
  - **Color perception**: Languages differ in color term boundaries; speakers faster at discriminating colors across their language's boundaries
  - **Spatial reference frames**: Some languages use absolute directions (north/south), others relative (left/right) → affects spatial memory and reasoning
  - **Grammatical gender**: German "bridge" (feminine) vs Spanish "bridge" (masculine) → Germans describe with feminine adjectives, Spanish with masculine

**Counter-evidence:**
- **Piaget**: Cognitive development precedes language
- **Deaf individuals without language** still have sophisticated thought
- Can think about concepts we lack words for

**Current View**: **Bidirectional influence**
- Language can make certain thoughts easier/more salient
- But doesn't create fundamental cognitive capacities
- **Thinking for speaking** (Slobin): Language shapes what we habitually attend to

---

### **Bilingualism and Cognition**

**Cognitive Benefits:**
- Enhanced executive control (inhibiting one language strengthens inhibitory control generally)
- Better at conflict resolution tasks (Stroop, Simon)
- **Metalinguistic awareness**: Better at thinking about language as object

**Code-switching:**
- Bilingual speakers seamlessly switch between languages
- Not random—follows grammatical and social rules
- Shows remarkable cognitive flexibility

---

## **Integration Across Domains: Case Study - Reading**

**Reading integrates perception, attention, memory, language:**

1. **Perception**: Extract letter features (visual processing)
2. **Attention**: Focus on fixation point, saccade to next (~3-4 words/second)
3. **Word recognition**: Letter features activate letter representations → word representations (interactive activation)
4. **Phonological recoding**: Convert orthography to phonology (especially for unfamiliar words)
5. **Semantic access**: Retrieve word meaning from semantic memory
6. **Syntactic parsing**: Build sentence structure incrementally
7. **Discourse integration**: Connect to prior text in working memory; build situation model in LTM
8. **Inference**: Fill gaps with world knowledge from LTM

**This happens in ~250ms per word**—testament to cognitive system's power.

# Developmental Psychology: Deep Mastery Roadmap

Developmental psychology studies **systematic changes** across the lifespan. The central question: How do humans transform from a single cell into complex beings capable of language, abstract thought, moral reasoning, and self-reflection—and how do we continue changing until death?

---

## **Foundational Frameworks: The Meta-Questions**

Before diving into stages, understand the **theoretical tensions** that structure all developmental thinking:

### **1. Nature vs. Nurture (Genes vs. Environment)**

**Modern Answer**: **Epigenetic interaction**—genes and environment are inseparable.

- **Gene-environment correlation**: Genes influence which environments you experience
  - *Passive*: Parents provide genes AND environment (musical parents → musical genes + music-rich home)
  - *Evocative*: Your genetically-influenced traits evoke responses (fussy baby → less parental warmth)
  - *Active*: You select environments matching your genetic predispositions (extroverted teen seeks social activities)

- **Gene-environment interaction**: Same genes have different effects in different environments
  - **MAO-A gene**: "Warrior gene"—low activity version linked to aggression, BUT only if childhood maltreatment present
  - High-quality environment can suppress genetic risk; poor environment can trigger it

**Mental Model**: Genes load the gun; environment pulls the trigger. Or better: Genes provide range of reaction; environment determines where in that range you land.

---

### **2. Continuity vs. Discontinuity (Gradual vs. Stage-like)**

**Continuous Development**: Quantitative changes—more of the same (like a ramp)
- Information processing theories emphasize gradual improvements in speed, capacity, strategies

**Discontinuous Development**: Qualitative changes—reorganization into new forms (like stairs)
- **Stage theories** (Piaget, Freud, Erikson): Distinct periods with unique characteristics
- Evidence for both: Some abilities emerge gradually; others show sudden transitions

**Resolution**: Depends on timescale and level of analysis
- Micro-level (neurons): Continuous synaptic strengthening
- Macro-level (behavior): Can appear stage-like (walking, language)

---

### **3. Stability vs. Change (How much do we remain the same?)**

**Critical question**: Does infant temperament predict adult personality? Do early experiences have permanent effects?

- **Canalization**: Self-correcting development—deviations pushed back to species-typical path
  - Example: Growth stunted by malnutrition → catch-up growth when nutrition improves
- **Sensitive periods**: Times when experience has greatest impact (but not absolute)
- **Cumulative continuity**: Personality becomes more stable with age (temperament → personality traits)

**Evidence**: Moderate stability across lifespan, but significant change possible
- Infant attachment security moderately predicts adult attachment (~30% variance)
- Personality traits crystallize in late 20s-30s (but still changeable)

---

### **4. Domain-General vs. Domain-Specific Development**

**Domain-general**: Single mechanism drives development across all areas
- Piaget: Logical operations apply to all content
- Working memory capacity increases affect all cognitive abilities

**Domain-specific**: Separate modules develop independently
- Language acquisition device (Chomsky)
- Theory of Mind module
- Number sense

**Current view**: **Both**—some general mechanisms (attention, processing speed) plus specialized systems for evolutionarily important domains (face recognition, language, social reasoning)

---

## **Part 1: Prenatal Development - The First Environment**

### **Three Periods of Prenatal Development**

**1. Germinal Period (0-2 weeks post-conception)**
- **Fertilization**: Sperm penetrates ovum → zygote (23 chromosome pairs)
- **Cell division**: Zygote → morula → blastocyst
- **Implantation** (~1 week): Blastocyst embeds in uterine wall
- **Vulnerability**: ~50% of conceptions fail to implant

**2. Embryonic Period (3-8 weeks)**
- **Critical period for organ formation** (organogenesis)
- **Neural tube** forms → brain and spinal cord
- Heart begins beating (3-4 weeks)
- Limb buds emerge
- **Maximum teratogen vulnerability**: Exposure to harmful agents causes major structural defects
  - Thalidomide (tranquilizer) → limb malformations
  - Alcohol → fetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD)
  - Timing matters: Same teratogen causes different defects at different embryonic stages

**3. Fetal Period (9 weeks-birth)**
- **Growth and refinement**: Organs mature, body proportions change
- **Brain development accelerates**:
  - Neurogenesis (neuron birth): Peak at 3-4 months (~250,000 neurons/minute)
  - Migration: Neurons travel to final positions
  - Differentiation: Neurons specialize
- **Sensory development**:
  - Hearing functional ~24 weeks (fetuses respond to external sounds)
  - Prefer mother's voice at birth (learned in utero)
- **Viability**: ~24 weeks (with intensive medical care)

---

### **Teratogens and Prenatal Environment**

**Dose-response relationship**: Greater exposure → greater harm
**Timing-dependent effects**: Same agent has different effects at different stages

**Major teratogens:**

1. **Alcohol**: Leading preventable cause of intellectual disability
   - **FASD**: Facial abnormalities, growth deficiency, brain damage, cognitive/behavioral problems
   - No safe amount established—abstinence recommended

2. **Tobacco**: Low birth weight, premature birth, SIDS risk, respiratory problems, ADHD

3. **Drugs**: 
   - Prescription (isotretinoin, anticonvulsants) → birth defects
   - Illicit (cocaine, opioids) → withdrawal symptoms, developmental delays

4. **Infections**:
   - **Rubella** (German measles): Deafness, heart defects, intellectual disability
   - **Zika virus**: Microcephaly (abnormally small head/brain)
   - **HIV**: Can be transmitted; antiretroviral treatment reduces risk

5. **Environmental toxins**:
   - **Lead**: Cognitive impairment, behavioral problems
   - **Mercury** (fish consumption): Neurological damage
   - **Radiation**: Cell death, mutations

**Maternal stress effects**:
- High cortisol crosses placenta → affects fetal brain development
- Linked to: Premature birth, low birth weight, emotional/behavioral problems in childhood
- Mechanism: Programming of HPA axis (stress response system)

**Protective factors**:
- **Folic acid** supplementation → prevents neural tube defects (spina bifida)
- Adequate nutrition, prenatal care
- Social support buffers stress effects

---

### **Behavioral Genetics: Heritability Estimates**

**Heritability (h²)**: Proportion of variance in trait due to genetic differences (within a population at a specific time)

**Critical understanding**:
- Heritability ≠ genetic determinism
- Says nothing about individual development (only population variance)
- Can change across contexts

**Typical heritabilities**:
- **Intelligence (IQ)**: ~50% in adults (increases from ~20% in infancy to ~80% in elderly)
  - Why increase? Gene-environment correlation—adults select environments matching genetic predispositions
- **Personality traits**: ~40-50%
- **Psychiatric disorders**: 30-80% depending on disorder
  - Schizophrenia: ~80%
  - Major depression: ~40%
  - Autism: ~70-90%

**Methods**:
- **Twin studies**: Compare MZ (identical, 100% shared genes) vs DZ (fraternal, 50% shared genes)
- **Adoption studies**: Separate genetic from environmental influences
- **Molecular genetics**: Identify specific genes (usually many genes of small effect—polygenic)

## **Part 2: Infancy and Toddlerhood (0-2 years) - Foundations**

### **Physical and Motor Development**

**Cephalocaudal principle**: Head → feet development
**Proximodistal principle**: Center → extremities development

**Motor milestones** (average ages—wide individual variation):
- 2 months: Lifts head when prone
- 4 months: Rolls over
- 6 months: Sits without support
- 8-10 months: Crawls
- 12 months: Stands alone, may walk
- 15 months: Walks well

**Dynamic systems theory** (Thelen): Motor development = self-organization from multiple components
- Not just neural maturation—involves strength, body proportions, motivation, environmental opportunities
- Example: Stepping reflex disappears ~2 months (not neural immaturity—legs too heavy; support infant in water → stepping returns)

---

### **Brain Development in Infancy**

**Structural changes**:
- Birth: ~25% of adult brain weight
- 2 years: ~75% of adult weight
- Growth from: Synaptogenesis, myelination, glial cell proliferation

**Synaptogenesis** (synapse formation):
- **Exuberant overproduction**: Far more synapses than adult brain
- Peak: Visual cortex ~4-8 months; prefrontal cortex ~12 months
- **Synaptic pruning**: "Use it or lose it"—frequently used connections strengthened; unused pruned
  - Experience-expectant: Brain expects certain experiences (visual input, language) → use to sculpt itself
  - Experience-dependent: Unique experiences create new synapses

**Myelination**:
- Fatty sheath around axons → faster neural transmission
- Sensory/motor areas first; prefrontal cortex last (continues into 20s)

**Critical insight**: Infant brain = learning machine optimized for environmental input

---

### **Perceptual Development**

**Visual acuity**:
- Birth: ~20/400 (legally blind by adult standards)
- 6 months: ~20/60
- 12 months: ~20/20
- Improvement from: Retinal maturation, neural pathway development

**Face perception**:
- Innate preference for face-like patterns (even newborns look longer at faces)
- 3-4 months: Discriminate mother's face from strangers
- **Perceptual narrowing**: Initially discriminate all faces (human, monkey, own-race, other-race) → specialize in frequently encountered faces
  - 6 months: Equal at discriminating own-race and other-race faces
  - 9 months: Better at own-race (expertise developed)
  - **"Other-race effect"**: Adults worse at recognizing other-race faces (lack of perceptual tuning)

**Depth perception**:
- **Visual cliff** (Gibson & Walk): Infants ~6 months avoid "deep" side
- Requires crawling experience (locomotion changes fear of heights)

**Intermodal perception**:
- Integrating information across senses
- Newborns: Turn toward source of sound (audio-visual coordination)
- 4 months: Match lip movements to sounds (multisensory integration)

---

### **Cognitive Development: Piaget's Sensorimotor Stage (0-2 years)**

**Piaget's theory**: Children construct understanding through **active exploration**

**Core mechanism**: **Adaptation** via:
- **Assimilation**: Interpret new experiences using existing schemas
- **Accommodation**: Modify schemas to fit new experiences
- **Equilibration**: Balance between assimilation and accommodation

---

**Six Substages of Sensorimotor Stage:**

**1. Reflexive schemes (0-1 month)**
- Innate reflexes: Sucking, grasping, rooting
- No intentional behavior

**2. Primary circular reactions (1-4 months)**
- Repeat pleasurable actions centered on own body
- Example: Thumb sucking (discovered by chance → repeated)

**3. Secondary circular reactions (4-8 months)**
- Repeat actions that produce interesting effects on environment
- Example: Shake rattle → hear sound → shake again
- Still no clear means-end separation

**4. Coordination of secondary circular reactions (8-12 months)**
- **Intentional, goal-directed behavior** emerges
- Combine schemas to achieve goals
- Example: Push obstacle aside to reach toy
- **Object permanence** begins: Understand objects exist when not visible
  - **A-not-B error**: Search for hidden object at previous location (A) even after seeing it hidden at new location (B)
  - Reflects immature frontal lobe (inhibitory control)

**5. Tertiary circular reactions (12-18 months)**
- **Experimentation**: Vary actions to see different outcomes
- "Little scientists" actively explore
- Example: Drop spoon from different heights, positions to see what happens

**6. Mental representation (18-24 months)**
- **Symbolic thought** emerges
- Can mentally represent objects, actions
- **Deferred imitation**: Imitate observed behavior after delay (requires mental representation)
- **Pretend play** begins: Banana as telephone

---

**Object permanence** (understanding objects continue to exist when out of sight):

**Piaget's view**: Develops gradually across sensorimotor stage
- Before 8 months: "Out of sight, out of mind"
- 8-12 months: Search for hidden objects (A-not-B errors)
- 12+ months: Successful invisible displacement (track object through multiple hiding locations)

**Critiques** (Baillargeon and others):
- **Violation of expectation paradigm**: Infants look longer at impossible events
- 3-4 months: Show surprise when object disappears behind screen and doesn't reappear when screen removed
- *Implication*: Some object permanence understanding much earlier than Piaget claimed
- **Resolution**: Distinction between **implicit knowledge** (what infants know) and **explicit performance** (what they can do)
  - Infants have early perceptual expectations but lack motor/planning skills to demonstrate in search tasks

---

### **Language Development**

**Prelinguistic period (0-12 months)**:

**Categorical perception**: Distinguish phoneme boundaries
- Newborns: Discriminate phonemes from all languages (universal phoneme sensitivity)
- 6-12 months: **Perceptual narrowing**—lose ability to discriminate non-native phoneme contrasts, improve on native contrasts
  - Japanese infants ~6 months: Discriminate /r/ vs /l/ (not distinct in Japanese)
  - 12 months: Can't discriminate (perceptual system tuned to Japanese)

**Vocalizations**:
- 2 months: **Cooing** (vowel-like sounds: "oooh," "ahhh")
- 6-8 months: **Babbling** (consonant-vowel combinations: "bababa," "dadada")
  - **Canonical babbling**: Universal across languages initially
  - Then shaped by language exposure (babbling becomes language-specific)
- Deaf infants: Babble with hands (manual babbling) if exposed to sign language

**Comprehension precedes production**: Understand words before can say them

---

**Linguistic period (12+ months)**:

**12-18 months: First words**
- **Holophrastic speech**: One word conveys whole sentence ("Mama" = "Mama, come here")
- **Underextension**: Word applied too narrowly (only family dog is "doggy")
- **Overextension**: Word applied too broadly (all four-legged animals are "doggy")

**18-24 months: Vocabulary explosion**
- Rapid word learning (~10-20 new words per week)
- **Fast mapping**: Form quick, rough meaning from minimal exposure
- **Mutual exclusivity bias**: Assume each object has one name
- **Whole object bias**: Assume word refers to whole object, not part

**24+ months: Two-word combinations**
- **Telegraphic speech**: Content words only ("More juice," "Daddy go")
- Grammatical function words (articles, prepositions) omitted
- Shows understanding of word order, basic grammar

**3-5 years: Grammatical explosion**
- Rapid acquisition of complex syntax
- **Overregularization**: Apply rules too broadly ("goed," "mouses")
  - Evidence children extracting grammatical rules, not just imitating
- By age 5: Basic grammar largely mastered (~90% correct)

---

**Theoretical debates**:

**Nativist view (Chomsky)**:
- **Language Acquisition Device (LAD)**: Innate module for language
- **Universal Grammar**: Deep structure common to all languages
- Evidence: Speed of acquisition, poverty of stimulus (input doesn't fully explain output), critical period, cross-linguistic universals

**Interactionist view**:
- Language emerges from interaction of:
  - Innate cognitive biases (pattern recognition, social cognition)
  - Social interaction (joint attention, pointing, turn-taking)
  - Environmental input (child-directed speech)
- Less domain-specific than nativists claim

**Statistical learning**:
- Infants extract statistical regularities from input
- 8-month-olds: Detect which syllables co-occur frequently → segment continuous speech into words
- Suggests powerful general learning mechanisms (not just language-specific)

**Current consensus**: Both innate constraints AND environmental input essential

---

## **Part 3: Attachment Theory - The Foundation of Relationships**

### **Bowlby's Attachment Theory**

**Core premise**: Infants are biologically predisposed to form attachments to caregivers for **survival**

**Evolutionary function**: Proximity to caregiver → protection from predators, nourishment, learning

**Key concepts**:

1. **Attachment behavioral system**: Innate system that monitors caregiver proximity, activates when threatened
   - **Activation**: Separation, fear, illness → proximity-seeking behaviors (crying, clinging, following)
   - **Deactivation**: Caregiver proximity, comfort → exploration

2. **Internal working models**: Mental representations of:
   - Self (worthy of care vs unworthy)
   - Others (trustworthy vs unreliable)
   - Relationships (secure base vs unpredictable)
   - Formed based on caregiver responsiveness
   - Guide expectations and behavior in future relationships

3. **Secure base**: Caregiver provides safe haven to return to and base from which to explore
   - Secure attachment: Confident exploration knowing caregiver available
   - Insecure attachment: Either clingy (can't explore) or avoidant (don't seek comfort)

---

### **Ainsworth's Strange Situation (Assessment of Attachment)**

**Procedure** (laboratory, ~20 minutes, 12-18 month infants):
1. Parent and infant in playroom
2. Stranger enters
3. Parent leaves (first separation)
4. Parent returns, stranger leaves (first reunion)
5. Parent leaves, infant alone (second separation)
6. Stranger returns
7. Parent returns (second reunion)

**Coding**: Infant's behavior during separations and especially reunions

---

**Attachment Classifications:**

**1. Secure (Type B) - ~60-65% in US samples**
- **Separation**: May or may not cry, but clearly prefers parent to stranger
- **Reunion**: Greet parent positively, seek comfort if distressed, quickly soothed, return to play
- **Exploration**: Use parent as secure base
- **Caregiver**: Sensitive, responsive, consistent
- **Internal working model**: "I am worthy of care; others are reliable"

**2. Insecure-Avoidant (Type A) - ~20%**
- **Separation**: Little distress, may ignore parent
- **Reunion**: Avoid or ignore parent, turn away, no comfort-seeking
- **Exploration**: Appear independent, but elevated cortisol (stressed despite appearing calm)
- **Caregiver**: Rejecting, dismissing of emotional needs, uncomfortable with intimacy
- **Internal working model**: "Others are unavailable; I must be self-reliant; emotions are dangerous"
- **Adaptive strategy**: Minimize attachment behavior to maintain proximity to rejecting caregiver

**3. Insecure-Resistant/Ambivalent (Type C) - ~10-15%**
- **Separation**: Extremely distressed before separation begins (heightened anxiety)
- **Reunion**: Simultaneous comfort-seeking and angry resistance (seek closeness but push away, hard to soothe)
- **Exploration**: Clingy, little exploration even when parent present
- **Caregiver**: Inconsistent—sometimes responsive, sometimes not; unpredictable
- **Internal working model**: "I am not worthy unless I maximize attachment behavior; others are unreliable"
- **Adaptive strategy**: Maximize attachment behavior to ensure inconsistent caregiver pays attention

**4. Disorganized (Type D) - ~15% (higher in maltreated samples)**
- **Behavior**: Contradictory, disoriented, confused, sometimes freezing
  - Approach parent with head averted
  - Seek proximity to stranger instead of parent
  - Dazed expression, stilling
- **Caregiver**: Frightening or frightened
  - Maltreatment, severe depression, unresolved trauma
- **Internal working model**: "Caregiver is source of both comfort and fear" → unsolvable dilemma
- **Highest risk**: Psychopathology, especially borderline personality disorder, dissociation

---

### **Determinants of Attachment Security**

**1. Caregiver sensitivity**: Most important predictor
- Prompt, appropriate responses to infant signals
- Correctly interpret cues
- Consistent availability

**2. Infant temperament**: Modest contribution
- **Temperament**: Biologically-based individual differences in reactivity and self-regulation
  - Easy (40%): Positive mood, regular rhythms, adaptable
  - Difficult (10%): Negative mood, irregular, intense reactions
  - Slow-to-warm-up (15%): Low activity, mild reactions, initially negative but gradually adaptive
- Temperament doesn't determine attachment, but interacts with caregiving
  - Difficult temperament + insensitive parenting → insecure attachment
  - Difficult temperament + sensitive parenting → often secure attachment

**3. Goodness of fit**: Match between child's temperament and environment

**4. Cultural context**: 
- Attachment security universal, but distribution varies
- German infants: Higher avoidance (cultural value on independence)
- Japanese infants: Higher resistance (rarely separated from mother)

---

### **Consequences of Attachment Security**

**Longitudinal predictions** (modest but meaningful):

**Secure attachment associated with**:
- Better emotion regulation
- More positive peer relationships
- Higher self-esteem
- Greater empathy, prosocial behavior
- Lower rates of psychopathology

**Insecure attachment (especially disorganized) associated with**:
- Externalizing problems (aggression, defiance)
- Internalizing problems (anxiety, depression)
- Difficulty with intimate relationships in adulthood

**BUT**: Not destiny—attachment can change based on life circumstances
- Secure → insecure: Major family stress, trauma
- Insecure → secure: Improved caregiving, therapy, supportive relationships

---

### **Adult Attachment**

**Adult Attachment Interview (AAI)** - assesses current state of mind regarding early attachment experiences

**Classifications parallel infant types**:

1. **Secure/Autonomous**: Coherent, balanced narratives; value attachment relationships
2. **Dismissing**: Idealize or devalue parents; claim independence; suppress negative memories
3. **Preoccupied**: Confused, angry, passive; still entangled with past experiences
4. **Unresolved/Disorganized**: Lapses in reasoning when discussing loss or trauma

**Intergenerational transmission**: Parent's AAI classification predicts infant's Strange Situation classification (~75% correspondence)
- Mechanism: Parent's internal working model → parenting sensitivity → infant attachment

**Romantic relationships**:
- Secure: Comfortable with intimacy and independence
- Avoidant: Uncomfortable with closeness, value independence
- Anxious: Crave intimacy, fear abandonment, preoccupied with relationships

---

## **Part 4: Cognitive Development Across the Lifespan**

### **Piaget's Theory: Stage Model**

**Universal, invariant sequence of qualitatively distinct stages**

---

**Preoperational Stage (2-7 years)**

**Advances from sensorimotor**:
- **Symbolic representation**: Use symbols (words, images) to represent objects/events
- **Pretend play**: Elaborate fantasy scenarios
- **Deferred imitation**: Imitate behaviors observed days earlier

**Limitations** (why "pre-operational"—can't perform mental operations):

**1. Egocentrism**: Difficulty taking others' perspectives
- **Three mountains task**: Child describes scene from own view, can't describe from other viewpoints
- Not selfishness—cognitive limitation in perspective-taking

**2. Centration**: Focus on single, perceptually salient dimension
- **Conservation tasks**: Fail to understand that quantity unchanged despite perceptual transformation

**Conservation of liquid**:
- Show two identical glasses with equal liquid
- Pour one into taller, thinner glass
- "Which has more?" → "Tall one" (focus on height, ignore width)
- Can't **reverse** mental operations (imagine pouring back)
- Can't **decenter** (consider height AND width simultaneously)

**Other conservation tasks**:
- Number: Spread out row of coins → think more coins
- Mass: Roll clay ball into snake → think more clay
- Length: Move sticks apart → think longer

**3. Animism**: Attribute life to inanimate objects
- "Is the sun alive?" → "Yes, it moves"

**4. Irreversibility**: Can't mentally reverse operations

---

**Concrete Operational Stage (7-11 years)**

**Advances**: Can perform **mental operations** on concrete (tangible) objects/events

**Achievements**:

**1. Conservation**: Understand quantity unchanged by perceptual transformation
- Understand **identity** (nothing added/removed)
- Understand **reversibility** (can pour back)
- **Decentration** (consider multiple dimensions)

**2. Classification**: 
- **Class inclusion**: Understand hierarchical relationships
  - "More roses or flowers?" → "Flowers" (roses are subset)
  - Preoperational: Answer "roses" (compare roses to non-rose flowers)
- **Seriation**: Order objects along quantitative dimension (smallest to largest)

**3. Transitivity**: Logical inference
- "A > B, B > C → A > C"

**Limitations**:
- Operations limited to concrete, tangible objects
- Struggle with abstract, hypothetical concepts

---

**Formal Operational Stage (11+ years)**

**Advances**: **Abstract, hypothetical, systematic thinking**

**Achievements**:

**1. Hypothetico-deductive reasoning**:
- Generate hypotheses, systematically test
- **Pendulum problem**: What determines swing rate (string length, weight, height, push force)?
  - Formal operational: Systematically vary one factor at a time (scientific method)
  - Concrete operational: Unsystematic trial-and-error

**2. Propositional thought**:
- Reason about verbal statements without concrete referents
- "If all fleebs are glorps, and this is a fleeb, is it a glorp?" → Yes

**3. Combinatorial thinking**:
- Systematically consider all possible combinations

**4. Metacognition**:
- Think about own thinking processes
- Monitor comprehension, regulate learning

**Note**: Not all adults reach formal operations (cross-cultural variation, depends on education/experience)

---

### **Critiques and Revisions of Piaget**

**Piaget underestimated infants and young children**:

**1. Object permanence** (as discussed): Emerges earlier than Piaget claimed

**2. Theory of Mind** (understanding others have mental states):
- **False belief understanding**: Recognize others can hold beliefs different from reality
- **Sally-Anne task**:
  - Sally puts marble in basket, leaves
  - Anne moves marble to box
  - "Where will Sally look for marble?" 
  - Correct: Basket (Sally doesn't know it moved)
  - Children <4: Say box (don't understand Sally's false belief)
- Piaget: Not until 7+ years
- Research: Implicit understanding at 15 months (looking time measures)

**3. Core knowledge systems** (Spelke):
- Infants have innate knowledge in core domains:
  - **Objects**: Solidity, continuity, contact
  - **Number**: Small number discrimination, approximate numerosity
  - **Agents**: Goal-directed action, intentionality
  - **Space**: Geometric relationships, navigation

**Piaget overestimated adolescents and adults**:
- Many adults don't use formal operations consistently
- Expertise and domain knowledge crucial (even adults think concretely in unfamiliar domains)

**Domain-specificity**: Development uneven across domains
- Child might conserve number but not liquid (Piaget predicted uniform stage)

**Social/cultural context neglected**: Piaget focused on individual child, ignored social interaction and cultural tools

---

### **Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory**

**Core premise**: Cognitive development = **socially mediated** process

**Key concepts**:

**1. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)**:
- Gap between:
  - What child can do independently (actual developmental level)
  - What child can do with guidance (potential developmental level)
- **Optimal learning**: Tasks within ZPD (neither too easy nor too hard)

**2. Scaffolding**:
- Temporary support provided by more knowledgeable other
- Gradually withdrawn as child becomes competent
- Example: Parent solving puzzle with child
  - Initially: Direct instruction, modeling
  - Later: Hints, questions
  - Finally: Child independent

**3. Private speech**:
- Talking to self to guide behavior
- Peak: Ages 4-6
- Piaget: Egocentric, cognitively immature
- Vygotsky: Self-regulation tool, eventually internalized as inner speech
- Evidence: Increases when tasks difficult (regulatory function)

**4. Cultural tools**:
- Language, writing, mathematics, maps—cultural inventions that amplify cognition
- Development = mastering cultural tools
- Cross-cultural differences in cognition reflect different tool use

**Comparison with Piaget**:
- Piaget: Child as scientist (individual discovery)
- Vygotsky: Child as apprentice (guided participation)
- Both right: Development requires individual construction AND social guidance

---

### **Information Processing Approach**

**Computer metaphor**: Mind processes information like computer (encoding, storage, retrieval, operations)

**Focus**: Continuous, gradual improvements in **cognitive processes**:

**1. Processing speed**: Faster with age
- Reaction time decreases from childhood through adolescence
- Allows more complex operations in limited time

**2. Attention**: More selective, sustained, flexible
- Infants: Easily distracted
- School-age: Can focus despite distractions

**3. Memory strategies**:
- **Rehearsal**: Repeat information (emerges ~7 years)
- **Organization**: Group related items (emerges ~9-10 years)
- **Elaboration**: Create meaningful connections (emerges adolescence)

**4. Metacognition**: Knowledge about own cognition
- Improves dramatically in middle childhood
- Better study strategies, self-monitoring

**5. Knowledge base**: More knowledge → easier to encode/retrieve new related information
- **Expertise reverses age effects**: Child chess experts remember chess positions better than adult novices
- Knowledge organized into schemas, scripts

**6. Working memory capacity**: Increases from ~2 items (age 2) to ~7 items (adulthood)
- Debated: True capacity increase vs. processing speed/efficiency?

---

### **Executive Functions**

**Higher-order control processes** (prefrontal cortex):

**1. Inhibitory control**: Suppress prepotent responses
- **Stroop task**: Name ink color of color words (blue printed in red → say "red")
- Improves through childhood and adolescence

**2. Cognitive flexibility**: Switch between tasks/perspectives
- **Dimensional Change Card Sort**: Sort by one dimension (color), then switch (shape)
- 3-year-olds: Perseverate on first rule
- 5-year-olds: Successfully switch

**3. Working memory**: Hold and manipulate information

**Development**: Executive functions improve through childhood/adolescence
- Linked to prefrontal cortex maturation (last brain region to mature, continues into mid-20s)

---

## **Part 5: Social and Emotional Development**

### **Emotional Development**

**Primary emotions** (universal, innate): Joy, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust
- Present at birth or emerge in first 6 months

**Self-conscious emotions**: Require self-awareness and understanding of standards
- **Embarrassment, pride, shame, guilt**: Emerge 18-24 months
- Coincides with mirror self-recognition (pass rouge test—notice mark on own face in mirror)

**Emotion regulation**:
- Infancy: Caregiver regulation (soothing)
- Toddlerhood: Co-regulation (caregiver support + child's emerging strategies—thumb sucking, looking away)
- Preschool: Independent strategies (distraction, self-talk)
- School-age: Cognitive reappraisal (reinterpret situation)

---

### **Temperament**

**Thomas & Chess**: Individual differences in reactivity and self-regulation

**Dimensions** (Rothbart):
1. **Negative affectivity**: Fearfulness, anger, sadness
2. **Surgency/extraversion**: Activity level, approach, positive anticipation
3. **Effortful control**: Attention focusing, inhibitory control (overlaps with executive functions)

**Stability**: Moderate from infancy to adulthood
- Infant fearfulness → childhood behavioral inhibition → adult introversion/neuroticism
- Infant effortful control → childhood conscientiousness

**Biological basis**:
- Genetic (~40-60% heritability)
- Amygdala reactivity (fearfulness), dopaminergic systems (approach), prefrontal cortex (effortful control)

---

### **Gender Development**

**Sex differences** (biological) vs **gender differences** (social/psychological):

**Cognitive differences** (small, overlapping distributions):
- Males: Slightly better spatial rotation, mathematical reasoning (at high end)
- Females: Slightly better verbal fluency, reading comprehension, social cognition

**Social/personality differences**:
- Females: More empathy, prosocial behavior, internalizing problems
- Males: More aggression (especially physical), risk-taking, externalizing problems

**Theories of gender development**:

**1. Social learning theory**: Learn through observation, reinforcement
- Parents, peers, media reinforce gender-typed behavior

**2. Cognitive-developmental theory** (Kohlberg):
- **Gender identity** (~2-3 years): Label self as boy/girl
- **Gender stability** (~4 years): Understand gender constant over time
- **Gender constancy** (~5-7 years): Understand gender unchanged by appearance/activities
- After constancy: Strongly identify with gender, seek same-gender models

**3. Gender schema theory**: Gender schemas (cultural associations) guide processing
- Notice and remember schema-consistent information
- Self-socialize based on schemas

**4. Biological factors**:
- Prenatal hormones (testosterone) organize brain
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH): Genetic females exposed to high prenatal androgens → more male-typical play preferences

**Current view**: **Biopsychosocial interaction**
- Biological predispositions + social amplification

---

### **Peer Relations and Friendship**

**Developmental changes**:

**Early childhood**: 
- Parallel play → cooperative play
- Friendships based on proximity, shared activities

**Middle childhood**:
- Peer groups more important
- Same-gender friendships
- Friendship based on loyalty, trust
- **Peer acceptance**: Popular, rejected, neglected, controversial children
- Rejected-aggressive children: High risk for later problems

**Adolescence**:
- Peer influence peaks
- Intimate friendships (especially girls)
- Romantic relationships emerge
- **Identity formation**: Peer comparisons crucial

---

### **Moral Development**

**Kohlberg's theory**: Stage sequence based on moral reasoning (not behavior)

**Method**: Present moral dilemmas (Heinz dilemma: Steal drug to save dying wife?)

**Three levels, six stages**:

**Level 1: Preconventional** (focus on consequences to self)
- Stage 1: Obedience—avoid punishment
- Stage 2: Instrumental purpose—satisfy own needs

**Level 2: Conventional** (focus on social approval, maintaining order)
- Stage 3: "Good child"—please others, gain approval
- Stage 4: Law and order—uphold laws, fulfill duties

**Level 3: Postconventional** (focus on abstract principles)
- Stage 5: Social contract—laws are agreements, can be changed if unjust
- Stage 6: Universal ethical principles—individual conscience, justice, human rights

**Progression**: Invariant sequence (can't skip stages), universal across cultures (though rate varies)

**Critiques**:
- **Gender bias** (Gilligan): Emphasizes justice over care
  - Males: Focus on justice, rights, fairness
  - Females: Focus on relationships, care, responsibility
- **Cultural bias**: Collectivist cultures emphasize community responsibility (appear "lower" stage)
- **Reasoning vs. behavior**: Moral reasoning doesn't strongly predict moral action

---

## **Part 6: Adolescence - The Second Major Transition**

### **Puberty: Biological Changes**

**Hormonal changes**:
- **Adrenarche** (~6-8 years): Adrenal glands increase androgen production
- **Gonadarche** (~8-13 girls, 9-14 boys): Gonads (ovaries/testes) activated
- HPG axis (hypothalamus-pituitary-gonadal) maturation

**Physical changes**:
- **Primary sex characteristics**: Reproductive organs mature
- **Secondary sex characteristics**: Breasts, facial hair, voice deepening, body composition changes
- **Growth spurt**: Peak height velocity
  - Girls: ~11.5 years
  - Boys: ~13.5 years
- **Menarche** (first period): Average ~12.5 years (US)
- **Spermarche** (first ejaculation): Average ~13.5 years

**Timing effects**:
- **Early-maturing girls**: Higher risk for depression, eating disorders, substance use, early sexual activity
  - Mechanisms: Peer pressure (older friends), poor fit with age-mates, objectification
- **Late-maturing boys**: Lower self-esteem, peer rejection (initially—often catch up)

---

### **Brain Development in Adolescence**

**Dual systems model**:

**1. Limbic system** (emotion, reward):
- **Rapid maturation** in early adolescence
- **Nucleus accumbens** (reward center): Heightened dopamine response to rewards
- Increased reward-seeking, sensation-seeking, risk-taking

**2. Prefrontal cortex** (cognitive control):
- **Slow, protracted maturation** (continues into mid-20s)
- Executive functions still developing: Impulse control, planning, future-oriented thinking

**Imbalance**: "All accelerator, weak brakes"
- Explains adolescent risk-taking: High reward sensitivity + underdeveloped inhibitory control
- Especially risky in presence of peers (social reward amplification)

**Synaptic pruning**: Adolescent brain undergoes second wave of pruning
- "Use it or lose it"—environment sculpts brain
- Gray matter volume decreases, white matter increases (myelination)

**Sleep changes**:
- Circadian rhythm shifts later (biological, not just laziness)
- Need ~9 hours but get ~7 hours
- Early school start times = chronic sleep deprivation → academic, emotional, health problems

---

### **Cognitive Development in Adolescence**

**Advances**:
- Formal operational thinking (abstract, hypothetical)
- Metacognitive sophistication
- Relativistic thinking (recognize multiple perspectives)

**Adolescent egocentrism** (Elkind):
- **Imaginary audience**: Believe everyone focused on them
  - "Everyone will notice this pimple"
- **Personal fable**: Belief in own uniqueness, invulnerability
  - "Other people get pregnant/crash cars, not me"
- Contributes to risk-taking

---

### **Identity Development (Erikson)**

**Psychosocial crisis**: **Identity vs. Role Confusion**

**Identity**: Coherent sense of self—values, beliefs, goals
- Involves exploration and commitment

**Marcia's identity statuses** (based on exploration and commitment):

1. **Identity Achievement**: Explored options, made commitments
   - Highest psychological well-being
   
2. **Identity Moratorium**: Actively exploring, haven't committed
   - Temporary confusion but positive long-term
   
3. **Identity Foreclosure**: Committed without exploration (adopted parents' values)
   - Rigid, dogmatic
   
4. **Identity Diffusion**: Neither exploring nor committed
   - Lowest well-being, directionless

**Ethnic identity**: Particularly important for minority adolescents
- Exploration of ethnic heritage
- Strong ethnic identity → better psychological adjustment

---

### **Parent-Adolescent Relationships**

**Conflict increases**: Peak in early adolescence
- Usually about mundane issues (chores, curfew, appearance)
- Reflects autonomy-seeking, identity formation
- Most maintain positive relationships

**Parenting styles** (Baumrind):

**1. Authoritative** (high warmth, high control):
- Responsive AND demanding
- Explain rules, encourage independence within limits
- **Best outcomes**: Academic achievement, social competence, psychological adjustment

**2. Authoritarian** (low warmth, high control):
- Demanding but not responsive
- "Because I said so"
- Outcomes: Obedient but lower self-esteem, social skills

**3. Permissive** (high warmth, low control):
- Responsive but not demanding
- Few rules, avoid confrontation
- Outcomes: Impulsive, poor self-regulation

**4. Uninvolved** (low warmth, low control):
- Neither responsive nor demanding
- Disengaged
- **Worst outcomes**: Academic, social, emotional problems

**Cultural note**: Authoritarian parenting less harmful in some cultures (collectivist, dangerous neighborhoods)

---

## **Part 7: Emerging Adulthood (18-25) - A New Stage**

**Arnett's "emerging adulthood"**: Distinct developmental period in industrialized societies

**Features**:
1. **Identity exploration**: Especially in love, work
2. **Instability**: Frequent changes in residence, relationships, jobs
3. **Self-focus**: Fewer social obligations than adolescence or adulthood
4. **Feeling in-between**: Not adolescent, not fully adult
5. **Possibilities**: Optimism about future

**Brain development**: Prefrontal cortex maturation continues
- Improved impulse control, planning, perspective-taking
- Risk-taking declines

**Life transitions**:
- Higher education
- Career exploration
- Cohabitation, marriage (delayed relative to previous generations)
- Parenthood (delayed)

---

## **Part 8: Adulthood and Aging**

### **Early Adulthood (25-40)**

**Physical peak**: Late 20s for most abilities
- Then gradual decline begins (barely noticeable initially)

**Cognitive abilities**:
- **Fluid intelligence** (novel problem-solving, processing speed): Peaks ~20s
- **Crystallized intelligence** (accumulated knowledge, vocabulary): Increases through middle age

**Psychosocial development** (Erikson):
- **Intimacy vs. Isolation**: Form close relationships, romantic partnership
- Failure → loneliness, isolation

**Life events**:
- Career establishment
- Marriage/partnership
- Parenthood (major life transition—sleep deprivation, role changes, relationship stress, but generally increased life satisfaction)

---

### **Middle Adulthood (40-65)**

**Physical changes**:
- Gradual decline in sensory acuity, reaction time, muscle mass
- Women: **Menopause** (~51 years)—end of fertility, hormone changes
  - Hot flashes, mood changes (variable experience)
- Men: Gradual testosterone decline (more gradual than menopause)

**Cognitive changes**:
- **Fluid intelligence**: Continues declining
- **Crystallized intelligence**: Maintained or improved
- **Processing speed**: Slows
- **Working memory**: Declines
- **Expertise**: Peaks—vast knowledge, pattern recognition compensates for processing decline

**Psychosocial** (Erikson):
- **Generativity vs. Stagnation**: Contribute to next generation (parenting, mentoring, creative work)
- **Midlife crisis**: Popularized but not universal (most navigate smoothly)

---

### **Late Adulthood (65+)**

**Physical aging**:
- Continued sensory decline (vision, hearing)
- Slower movement, reduced strength
- Increased chronic health conditions
- Brain volume decreases (especially hippocampus, prefrontal cortex)

**Cognitive aging**:

**Normal aging**:
- **Processing speed**: Significantly slower
- **Working memory**: Declines
- **Episodic memory**: Declines (remembering specific events)
- **Semantic memory**: Relatively preserved (general knowledge, vocabulary)
- **Procedural memory**: Well-preserved (skills)
- **Tip-of-the-tongue** states increase

**Individual differences**: HUGE variability
- Some 80-year-olds cognitively sharp
- Others show significant decline

**Protective factors**:
- Education (cognitive reserve)
- Physical exercise (neurogenesis, cardiovascular health)
- Cognitive engagement ("use it or lose it")
- Social engagement
- Healthy diet

**Pathological aging**:
- **Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI)**: Greater than normal decline, not yet dementia
- **Dementia**: Severe cognitive decline interfering with daily functioning
  - **Alzheimer's disease**: Most common (~60-70% of dementia cases)
    - Amyloid plaques, neurofibrillary tangles
    - Memory loss, disorientation, personality changes
    - No cure; treatments slow progression

**Psychosocial** (Erikson):
- **Integrity vs. Despair**: Reflect on life—satisfaction vs. regret
- **Life review**: Common in older adults—evaluate meaning, accomplishments

**Socioemotional selectivity theory** (Carstensen):
- Time perspective shifts: From open-ended to limited
- Goal priorities shift: From knowledge-acquisition to emotional meaning
- **Positivity effect**: Attend to and remember positive information more than negative
- Explains why older adults often report high well-being despite losses

---

## **Part 9: Critical and Sensitive Periods**

### **Critical Period**: Strict time window—if experience doesn't occur, development permanently impaired

**Examples**:

**1. Visual system development**:
- **Hubel & Wiesel**: Kittens with one eye sewn shut during critical period
  - Deprivation → permanent visual impairment in that eye
  - Critical period: ~3-8 weeks in cats
- **Humans**: Congenital cataracts—if not corrected before ~8 years, normal vision never fully develops
  - Even after removal: Poor visual acuity, face recognition

**2. Language acquisition**:
- **Lenneberg's Critical Period Hypothesis**: Language must be acquired before puberty
- **Evidence**: 
  - **Genie** (discovered age 13, no language exposure): Limited language development despite intensive intervention
  - **Deaf children and sign language**: If not exposed until adolescence, never achieve native fluency
  - **Second language acquisition**: After puberty, very difficult to achieve native-like pronunciation, grammar

**Mechanisms**: 
- Synaptic pruning—unused connections eliminated
- Brain plasticity decreases with age

---

### **Sensitive Period**: Optimal time window—easier to develop ability, but not impossible later

**Examples**:

**1. Attachment**:
- Optimal: First 2 years
- Late adoption (after age 2-3): More attachment difficulties, but secure attachment still possible with sensitive care

**2. Music**:
- Absolute pitch: Rare if training starts after age 7
- Musical skill generally: Easier to learn in childhood, but adults can still learn

**3. Second language**:
- Pronunciation, grammar: Sensitive period through adolescence
- Vocabulary: No sensitive period—can learn throughout life

---

### **Neuroplasticity Across the Lifespan**

**Plasticity = brain's ability to change structure/function in response to experience**

**High plasticity periods**:
1. **Prenatal-early childhood**: Massive synapse formation, experience-expectant development
2. **Adolescence**: Synaptic pruning, reorganization

**Mechanisms of plasticity**:

**1. Synaptogenesis**: New synapse formation
- Peaks in infancy
- Continues throughout life (especially hippocampus)

**2. Synaptic strengthening/weakening**:
- **Long-term potentiation (LTP)**: Strengthen connections (learning)
- **Long-term depression (LTD)**: Weaken connections (forgetting, refinement)

**3. Myelination**: Increase conduction speed
- Continues through adolescence, young adulthood

**4. Neurogenesis**: New neuron birth
- Occurs in hippocampus throughout life (but declines with age)
- Enhanced by: Exercise, enriched environment, learning
- Reduced by: Stress, sleep deprivation

**5. Reorganization**: 
- **Phantom limb**: After amputation, cortical area for missing limb taken over by adjacent areas
- **Blind individuals**: Visual cortex recruited for auditory, tactile processing

---

**Age-related changes in plasticity**:

**Infancy/childhood**: Maximum plasticity
- Rapid learning
- Greater recovery from brain injury
- But also: More vulnerable to adverse experiences

**Adolescence**: Selective plasticity
- Frontal lobe reorganization
- Still significant capacity for learning

**Adulthood**: Reduced but significant plasticity
- Can learn new skills, languages (more effort required)
- **Cognitive reserve**: Lifelong learning builds redundant neural networks → protects against age-related decline

**Older adulthood**: Further reduced but not absent
- London taxi drivers: Enlarged hippocampus from spatial navigation expertise (even starting in middle age)
- Learning new skills can slow cognitive decline

---

**Factors enhancing plasticity**:
1. **Enriched environment**: Varied stimulation, challenges, social interaction
2. **Physical exercise**: Increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) → neurogenesis
3. **Sleep**: Consolidates learning, prunes unnecessary connections
4. **Stress reduction**: Chronic stress impairs plasticity (cortisol damages hippocampus)

**Clinical implications**:
- **Stroke rehabilitation**: Intensive practice recruits intact areas to compensate
- **Developmental disorders**: Early intervention exploits high plasticity
- **Lifelong learning**: Maintains cognitive function in aging

---

## **Integration: Key Developmental Principles**

**1. Multidirectional**: Development involves gains AND losses at all ages
- Childhood: Gain abilities, lose flexibility (neural pruning)
- Aging: Lose processing speed, gain wisdom

**2. Multicontextual**: Multiple contexts influence development
- Microsystem (family), mesosystem (connections between contexts), exosystem (indirect contexts), macrosystem (culture), chronosystem (historical time)

**3. Cumulative**: Early experiences have cascading effects
- Insecure attachment → poor emotion regulation → peer rejection → antisocial behavior
- BUT: **Equifinality** (multiple paths to same outcome) and **multifinality** (same early experience, different outcomes)

**4. Probabilistic**: Risk/protective factors affect probabilities, not certainties
- No single factor determines outcome
- Resilience: Positive adaptation despite adversity

## **Developmental Psychology Mastery Protocol**

**1. Think Longitudinally**:
- Always ask: "How did this emerge?" and "Where is this going?"
- Connect across developmental periods

**2. Observe Children**:
- Watch infants, children at different ages
- Notice individual differences
- Test Piagetian tasks (try conservation with 4-year-old vs 7-year-old)

**3. Consider Multiple Levels**:
- Genes → brain → cognition → behavior → relationships → culture
- Development occurs at all levels simultaneously

**4. Evaluate Methods Critically**:
- Cross-sectional (compare different ages) vs longitudinal (follow same individuals)
- Age effects vs cohort effects
- Correlation vs causation (especially tricky in development)

**5. Integrate Theories**:
- No single theory explains everything
- Piaget: Cognitive construction
- Vygotsky: Social scaffolding
- Information processing: Mechanism of change
- Attachment: Emotional foundation
- Evolutionary psychology: Adaptive functions

**6. Apply to Self**:
- Reflect on own development
- Identify experiences that shaped you
- Notice continued development (you're not finished!)

**Critical Questions to Push Your Thinking**:

1. **Mechanism question**: HOW does this developmental change occur (neural, cognitive, social mechanisms)?

2. **Function question**: WHY did this develop (evolutionary function, adaptive value)?

3. **Timing question**: WHEN does this emerge, and why then (maturation, experience, critical period)?

4. **Individual differences question**: What causes VARIATION in developmental outcomes (genes, environment, their interaction)?

5. **Continuity question**: Does early X predict later Y, and if so, through what developmental pathway?
